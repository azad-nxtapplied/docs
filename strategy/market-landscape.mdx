## Key findings

- Enterprise AI has entered its accountability phase. The constraint on adoption has shifted from model capability to operational governance — and in regulated industries, regulation has turned that constraint into a procurement criterion with a date attached.

- Supply is converging on the problem from nine directions — core-system vendors, hyperscaler agent platforms, orchestration platforms, vertical AI applications, AI-native delivery startups, AI tooling, governance software, consultancies and SIs, and transformed BPO — yet each is structurally anchored to its origin. The convergence is real; the demand it aims at is still being served in fragments.

- A clearly specified demand remains unmet for the European regulated mid-market: validated process content, execution-level AI governance, and an accountable operating model, delivered together in the customer's own environment. Every supplier archetype serves a part of it; each is pulled away from the whole by its own business model.

- Buyer evaluation criteria are visibly maturing from "can it automate?" to "can it be attested?" — evidence-based autonomy, decision-level auditability, and a named accountable party for AI quality are becoming the questions that decide deals.

## 1. Market context: the production gap meets a regulatory deadline

The defining statistic of this market is not an adoption figure but a failure figure. Gartner predicts that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end-2027, citing escalating costs, unclear business value, and inadequate risk controls; IDC finds 88% of AI-agent proofs of concept never reach production, and Deloitte reports only 11% of organizations actively running agentic AI in production. The same Gartner research estimates that of the thousands of self-described agentic AI vendors, only around 130 possess genuine agentic capability — the remainder engaged in "agent washing." The market's core dysfunction, in other words, is not a shortage of AI; it is a shortage of the machinery that turns AI into an operation someone will stand behind.

In regulated industries this gap now has a calendar. The EU AI Act's high-risk obligations take effect on August 2, 2026; DORA governs ICT and third-party risk in financial services; supervisory practice (BaFin, FINMA — whose guidance has already been translated into technical assessment blueprints by Swiss vendors — and EIOPA equivalents) converges on the same expectations: know what the AI did and why, prove quality before and after change, keep humans in control of consequential decisions, evidence everything. Industry research confirms the diagnosis from the demand side: a 2026 Genpact/HFS study finds 92% of executives expect agentic AI to fundamentally change operations while naming organizational readiness — accountability, measurement, process — not technology as the primary barrier to scale.

A second, regional force shapes the European market specifically: sovereignty. The consolidation of Aleph Alpha into a ~\$20B combined entity with Cohere (Schwarz Group anchoring with \$600M), the rise of STACKIT as a German hyperscaler alternative, and the EU Cloud Sovereignty Framework all signal that *where* AI runs and *who controls the data* are first-order purchase criteria in this region — criteria that US-anchored platforms must answer for rather than assume away.

## 2. The demand side: seven paths a regulated buyer can actually take

For a regulated mid-market operations leader who has decided AI must move from pilot to production, the realistic choices are not "vendor A vs. vendor B" but seven structurally different paths. Most evaluations are won or lost at the path level before any vendor shortlist exists.

**Path 1 — Wait for the core system.** Extend what the core vendor will deliver: Temenos now embeds AI agents, copilots, and a conversational build studio across core banking and financial-crime products, with auditability and human oversight as explicit design points; SAP Fioneer — serving 1,200+ banks and insurers — ships a domain-specific AI Agent pre-integrated with its banking and insurance suites; Guidewire, Sapiens, the msg/adesso ecosystem in DACH insurance, and the broader ERP estate follow the same logic. The appeal is governance comfort and a single throat. The structural limit is equally clear: core vendors automate *inside their own perimeter*, while operational reality — exceptions, mailboxes, documents, cross-system coordination — lives between systems. Embedded AI raises the baseline; it does not run the casework.

**Path 2 — Adopt a hyperscaler agent platform.** Build on the agent stacks of the cloud incumbents — Microsoft (Foundry, Copilot Studio, the Agent Framework), Google (Gemini Enterprise, Vertex agent tooling), AWS (Bedrock AgentCore), increasingly Salesforce and ServiceNow inside their clouds. The appeal is overwhelming: existing enterprise agreements, ecosystem integration (an M365 estate makes Copilot agents feel free), and roadmap momentum. The limits, for a regulated operations buyer, are equally structural: these platforms are built for knowledge-worker productivity inside their own ecosystems, governance arrives as ecosystem-generic features rather than process-level controls, accountability for quality remains entirely the buyer's, and the sovereignty question — whose perimeter, whose jurisdiction — is sharpest precisely here. The hyperscalers define the baseline expectation for what "agents" means; they do not answer who attests to the casework.

**Path 3 — Self-develop.** The path engineering-led organizations instinctively prefer: own developers, open-source frameworks (LangGraph and successors), model APIs, perhaps an observability tool. The appeal is control and the absence of vendor dependency; the build looks deceptively close because demos come fast. The market data is the counterargument: 88% of agent proofs of concept never reach production, and the scarce skill is not prompt engineering but operations engineering — evidence pipelines, evaluation governance, autonomy control, audit-grade recording. Self-development means rebuilding precisely the undifferentiated 70% of the stack that regulation demands and no business case rewards, with key-person risk attached. It remains the right path for organizations whose core business is the software itself; for everyone else it is how pilots become legacy.

**Path 4 — Build on the orchestration estate.** Use the workflow platform already in the house — Camunda (whose ProcessOS now generates agentic process solutions), UiPath, ServiceNow, the hyperscaler agent stacks — and let internal engineering assemble the rest. The appeal is platform reuse and internal control. The limits are time, scarce AI engineering talent, and a subtle accountability inversion: the platform vendor sells the canvas; quality, evidence, and regulatory defensibility remain entirely the buyer's problem — including for AI logic the platform itself auto-generates. Autonomy on these platforms is a setting to configure, not a status earned on evidence.

**Path 5 — Buy a vertical application per process.** Strong specialist offerings exist, concentrated exactly where pain is acute: in Europe, Unique in financial-services workflows (Pictet, LGT, FINMA-aligned governance framework); Otera — formerly DeepOpinion — in insurance and banking back-office automation (Allianz Partners, HannoverRe, 90%+ straight-through-processing claims); omni:us, Shift Technology, Sprout.ai, and Tractable in claims; Parloa — Berlin, €310M Series D at a ~\$3B valuation in January 2026, with Allianz, Swiss Life, and Zurich Germany as customers — in customer-facing voice and service agents; Hyperexponential in underwriting; and a fast-forming agent layer in compliance itself: Bretton AI (formerly Greenlite, \$75M Series B) automating AML/KYC investigations, Norm Ai encoding regulation as executable logic, Taktile in decisioning. The appeal is fast, referenced value in one process. The limit appears at process number two: each vertical brings its own stack, its own controls, its own audit answer — governance fragments precisely where regulation demands it be uniform, and none of these vendors offers the operations discipline (evaluation-gated change, evidence-bound autonomy) as their product.

**Path 6 — Hand it to professional services.** This path has two faces, both transforming fast. The strategy and consulting firms have industrialized their AI arms: McKinsey's QuantumBlack (~1,700 specialists, 140+ use-case accelerators), BCG X (~3,000 technologists, formal Anthropic and OpenAI partnerships), Deloitte, Bain, and the Big Four all now sell AI build capability, not just advice. The appeal is board-level trust and breadth; the structural limits are the advisory model itself — hourly economics, 9–24-month paths to production, and the build-and-leave pattern: when the team rolls off, the customer owns a bespoke system and the entire quality burden, with no product compounding behind it. The second face is the transformed BPO: Capgemini's \$3.3B acquisition of WNS was framed explicitly as buying process knowledge to encode into agents; Accenture reports \$5.9B in agentic AI bookings in its last fiscal year; Genpact repositions as an "agentic solutions company" with offerings like accounts-payable-as-an-agent. Across both faces the appeal is one accountable provider; the structural limits for the regulated mid-market are shared: the model anchors on the provider's delivery organization and economics, the customer's data and process knowledge compound *outside* the customer (or leave with the project team), and the offering gravity points to global enterprise accounts.

**Path 7 — Commission an outcome on a platform.** The newest path, validated at venture scale in the US: Distyl (\$1.8B valuation) pairs forward-deployed engineers with a proprietary platform, prices partly on outcomes, and claims a 100% production success record in regulated sectors; Palantir AIP combines an "AI Operating System" positioning, evaluation and governance tooling, and forward deployment, now extending through managed-service partners into regulated industries. The appeal is exactly what the other paths lack: accountable, governed production AI. The limit, for the European mid-market, is altitude and provenance: both motions target the global enterprise, neither offers resellable regulated-process content for the mid-market, and US anchoring meets the region's sovereignty current head-on.

A new generation is compressing Path 7 toward the mid-market: AI-native delivery platforms that pair a proprietary platform with embedded delivery teams and outcome-oriented commercial models. Wonderful (founded 2025; \$150M Series B in March 2026 at a ~\$2B valuation; ~30 markets; locally embedded deployment teams; CX-first and expanding into adjacent enterprise workflows, including regulated industries) and Unframe (\$100M raised; \$100M in total contract value within twelve months; 400% net revenue retention; "turnkey" blueprint-based solutions in days; Berlin presence) prove both the demand and the model at startup velocity. Their gravity today is horizontal breadth and speed — customer-facing interaction and rapid use-case delivery — rather than regulated case operations with attestable governance; but they are the fastest-moving entrants on the board, and their trajectory crosses this market's center.

The honest eighth path — remaining in pilot purgatory — is statistically the most traveled, and the August 2026 enforcement date is what now forecloses it.

## 3. The supply side: six archetypes converging, each held back by its own gravity

Viewed from above, the market is a convergence story: every archetype is moving toward the same center — governed, production-grade, accountable AI operations — because that is where the unmet demand sits. Viewed closely, each is tethered.

| **Archetype** | **Representative motion toward the center** | **The gravity that holds it back** |
|----|----|----|
| Core-system & suite vendors | Temenos AI agents/copilots embedded in core banking with audit and oversight | Automates inside its own product perimeter; the cross-system casework remains outside |
| Hyperscaler agent platforms | Microsoft Foundry/Copilot Studio, Google Gemini Enterprise (agent platform launched at Cloud Next 2026), AWS Bedrock AgentCore, Salesforce Agentforce, ServiceNow — agents as an ecosystem feature | Ecosystem gravity: built for productivity inside their own clouds; generic governance; the buyer attests alone; sovereignty exposure in the EU |
| Orchestration & agent platforms | Camunda ProcessOS generating agentic processes; ServiceNow AI Control Tower; UiPath agentic automation | License model: the canvas is the product; quality and evidence remain the customer's burden |
| AI-native delivery platforms | Wonderful (\$2B, embedded local teams, 30 markets), Unframe (\$100M TCV in year one), Distyl (\$1.8B) — platform + delivery + outcome pricing | Horizontal speed over regulated depth: CX and generic use cases first; governance as enterprise hygiene, not as the product; playbook content thin where regulation is thick |
| AI engineering tooling | Eval-gated deployment, agent tracing, drift detection maturing fast (Braintrust, Langfuse — acquired by ClickHouse 1/2026, Arize, LangSmith) | Sold to developers as tools; no business semantics, no content, no operations accountability — commoditizing |
| AI governance software | Credo AI, Holistic AI, watsonx.governance, OneTrust, Monitaur, ModelOp; in Europe LatticeFlow plus a DACH boutique tier (Calvin Risk, Validaitor, trail) riding the AI-Act procurement wave | Documents and assesses beside the system; cannot produce execution-level primary evidence because it does not run the work |
| Vertical AI applications | Unique, Otera, omni:us, Shift, Sprout.ai, Parloa, Hyperexponential, Bretton, Norm Ai, Taktile — real regulated references, real content | One process per vendor; governance as feature, not product; no cross-process operations layer |
| Consultancies & SIs | McKinsey QuantumBlack, BCG X, Deloitte, Bain, IBM Consulting — strategy firms selling AI build at scale | Advisory economics: hourly model, long paths to production, build-and-leave; no compounding product |
| Transformed BPO & outcome-owned services | Capgemini/WNS, Accenture's agentic bookings, Genpact's repositioning; Distyl, Palantir+partners | Enterprise-scale delivery economics; knowledge compounds at the provider; mid-market motion absent |

Two second-order dynamics sharpen the picture. **Consolidation is sorting the stack:** infrastructure absorbs tooling (ClickHouse/Langfuse), sovereignty consolidates at the model layer (Cohere/Aleph Alpha/Schwarz), services absorb process knowledge (Capgemini/WNS), and the customer-service agent segment is consolidating outright (Zendesk/Forethought) — each move clarifying that the *layers below* and *beside* operations are being claimed, while operations itself is not. And **evaluation criteria are migrating upward in buyer organizations:** what was a developer-tooling question ("do we have evals?") is becoming a board-level attestation question ("can we evidence how this AI behaves?") — visible in IDC's commentary on the Temenos launch ("the critical question is no longer whether AI can be applied, but whether it can be governed across data lineage, model behavior, and operational controls") and in the FINMA-blueprint work emerging from Zurich.

## 4. The unmet demand

Compose the limits of §3 and the shape of the unmet demand becomes precise. The market currently offers regulated mid-market buyers: intelligence embedded in single systems (Path 1), canvases without accountability (Path 2), content without an operations platform (Path 3), accountability without customer-side compounding (Path 4), and the full combination — at enterprise altitude, with US anchoring (Path 5). What buyers and regulators are now jointly specifying — and what no archetype yet delivers as one offering (the hyperscalers bring distribution without process accountability; the delivery startups bring speed and embedded teams without regulated-operations depth; the professional-services firms bring accountability without a product that compounds) — is the combination of:

- 1\. **Validated process content** — the AI-native process design, skills, reference datasets, policies, and exception handling per regulated process, proven before deployment rather than designed per project;

- 2\. **Execution-level operations governance** — decision-level audit with full context capture, policy gates enforced in the execution path, autonomy bound to evaluation evidence, and change that cannot ship silently;

- 3\. **An operating model in the customer's perimeter** — the customer's environment, identity, and change process; the customer's accountability supported by attestable evidence; the provider engaged in the continuous quality cycle — with the customer's data and learning compounding inside the customer's walls.

Several vendors hold one element; a few hold two; the analysis of §2–§3 finds none delivering all three together for the European regulated mid-market. That combination is the unmet demand this report set out to test — and the finding stands with that precise scope, neither broader nor narrower.

## 5. NXT's position in this landscape

NXT — The AI Operations Company — is built on exactly this intersection: proven playbooks per (regulated) process, the AI Operations Platform NXT Core providing execution-level governance, and a design-build-run engagement model deployed in the customer's environment, focused on banks, insurers, and healthcare companies in the European mid-market. In the language of this report: NXT's proposition is built to answer this unmet demand in full rather than in fragments — using the commoditizing tooling layer as substrate, coexisting with orchestration estates and core systems rather than displacing them, and treating governance software and sovereignty infrastructure as adjacent layers to integrate with, not compete against.

The position carries obligations the market will test. Against vertical specialists, NXT must win on the platform beneath the playbook — one governance spine across all AI processes rather than one vendor per process. Against the build-it-here instinct, on time-to-attestable-evidence. Against transformed BPO, on where knowledge compounds. And against the enterprise outcome players, on fit: mid-market economics, weeks-not-quarters deployment, European perimeter. These are falsifiable claims; the current lighthouse deployments are their test.

## 6. Outlook

**Through 2026:** the August enforcement date functions as the market's forcing event; expect attestability requirements to appear in regulated-industry RFPs as standard sections, and governance-washing to follow agent-washing as the category's credibility problem.

**2027:** consolidation continues along the §3 lines — tooling into infrastructure, content into services, models into sovereignty blocs. The orchestration platforms will add evidence features (the gap is too visible to leave open); the question is whether bolted-on evidence can match evidence-by-construction in audit settings.

**The strategic watch items, in order:** Otera's governance messaging maturing (the nearest deal-level rival in DACH case operations); Wonderful extending from CX into regulated case operations on the strength of its embedded-team model — the entrant whose trajectory most directly crosses this layer, and equally the most natural partnership surface for those who hold the regulated-operations depth it lacks; ProcessOS adding evaluation evidence; Unique landing case-operations (beyond copilot) wins in German banking; Unframe's Berlin-based delivery motion meeting DACH mid-market demand; Palantir packaging AIP for the mid-market through partners; hyperscalers bundling sovereign agent operations with managed services — the move that would most reshape Path 2.

The summary judgment: the market has spent three years proving that AI capability is abundant and AI accountability is scarce. The vendors who industrialize accountability — as a running operation, not a document — will define the category. In Europe, with its regulatory calendar and sovereignty current, that definition is being written first.
