## 1. From positioning to architecture

NXT is The AI Operations Company. We make AI work in regulated industries — for banks, insurers, and healthcare companies — governed, attestable, and getting better every cycle. We stand on two assets: **proven playbooks** per key process, and **the AI Operations Platform — NXT Core**. We deliver two kinds of AI solutions: **regulated case operations** and **governed AI components embedded** in the customer's own software and workflows. We deploy in the customer's environment, on top of the customer's systems, and the customer's accountability is never taken — it is made carryable with evidence.

Each of these sentences is an architectural requirement. Read as engineering, the positioning demands:

- 1\. *"Attestable"* → every AI decision must be recorded with its complete context, tamper-evidently, and exportable as evidence. Not as a logging feature — as the structural property that nothing can happen without producing its own record.

- 2\. *"Governed"* → the customer's policies must be executable gates in the execution path, and AI autonomy must be a controlled, evidenced, reversible state — never a configuration default.

- 3\. *"Getting better every cycle"* → quality must be measured against reference data, every change to AI behavior must pass evaluation before release, and human corrections must flow back as validated improvements. No silent changes, ever — including to logic the platform itself generates.

- 4\. *"Proven playbooks"* → process knowledge must exist as versioned, testable, deployable artifacts — not as code woven into a project, and not as consulting documents beside it.

- 5\. *"Two kinds of solutions, one governance"* → an invisible API call from the customer's software must be governed exactly like a case on a clerk's screen: same ledger, same gates, same evidence.

- 6\. *"Your environment, your systems"* → the platform must deploy self-contained into the customer's perimeter, treat the customer's systems as the systems of record, and let NXT operate the AI quality cycle from outside on quality signals alone — never on business content.

The architecture below is the minimal structure that satisfies all six at once. It has four layers, and the layers are the two pillars made structural: **Layers 1 and 2 are the platform pillar; Layers 3 and 4 are the playbook pillar.**

## 2. The four layers

The layers are dependency rules, enforced in the build pipeline: a layer may depend on layers below it, never sideways into another vertical, never upward. The test that separates the bottom two: remove a Layer 1 element and a *guarantee* breaks; remove a Layer 2 element and a *way of working* breaks. Industry language first appears at Layer 3; the customer first appears at Layer 4.

**Layer 1 — Kernel.** The guarantees and the memory every deployment stands on: every action recorded with what the AI saw, policies enforced as gates, autonomy earned on evaluation evidence — and the data that makes all of it real: the case and execution records, the evidence store, the governed knowledge base, and the read-mirrors of the customer's systems with guaranteed freshness. At its heart sits the learning machinery: corrections, outcomes, and evaluation results are captured here and turned into validated improvements, so the kernel is not just where AI is controlled but where it gets better. It knows no industries and no workflows — and it is identical in every deployment, which is why compliance and learning are never custom-built.

**Layer 2 — AI Suites.** The two ways of working, assembled from kernel parts and exposed through one open, versioned Platform API — every capability accessible programmatically, with NXT's own Workspace as just another API consumer. Case Operations runs work end to end with people in the loop; Embedded AI Components let the customer's software call governed components and receive results with explanation, confidence, and evidence. And this is where command over the system lives: the NXT Cockpit, where humans on the loop watch quality trends, review samples of completed work, and decide — on evidence — when the AI earns more autonomy and where it must improve.

**Layer 3 — AI-native Playbooks.** Where the industry lives: per key process, the validated AI-native redesign — what AI does, where humans decide — together with the skills, golden datasets, policies, and exception rules, packaged, versioned, and release-gated. The playbook also defines its human side: the Workspace views, checkpoints, and review steps through which people work inside the process, because an AI-native process is a human-and-AI process by design. The second customer gets configuration, not a project.

**Layer 4 — Deployment.** The only layer that knows the customer: their parameter values, autonomy choices, connections, branding — and their own components: customer-specific or customer-proprietary solutions built on published contracts, owned by or exclusive to the customer, yet fully governed by Layers 1 and 2 — audited, evaluation-gated, learning and improving like everything else on the platform. Everything specific sits at the top so everything beneath stays standard, certifiable, and upgradeable. The IP line and the architecture line are the same line.

**Two terms, used precisely throughout:** a **Skill** is a capability given to the AI — a taught, versioned unit of work (classify a complaint, check billing completeness, draft a response). A **Component** is a solution built for a customer that runs on the platform — composed of skills, policies, and configuration. Components are built from skills; playbooks define and validate both.

## 3. Layer 1 — the Kernel, element by element

*Why these ten and no others: each exists because a sentence of the positioning is unbuildable without it.*

**Model Access — one governed inference, whatever the model. Every interaction with a model — a hosted language model or a trained ML model we serve ourselves — passes through one governed door: EU-region routing by default, provider and backend fallback, model version pinning, and token/latency/cost capture attributed to the case that caused it. Two backends, one governance wrapper: an LLM proxy for language models, and a model-serving runtime for NXT-trained models such as rankers and classifiers. Derived from: "your environment" (residency is enforced at the only place models are reached), "attestable" (which model and which version produced which result is recorded, always — for classical ML exactly as for LLMs), and economic transparency (cost per case is measured, not estimated). No application code may reach a model or provider directly. An ML inference is an Execution exactly like an LLM call: the platform governs AI decisions, not merely LLM calls.**

**Skill Runtime** — *one skill, bounded steps.* Skills are rarely single model calls: a completeness check retrieves rules, extracts, assesses, composes an explanation. The Skill Runtime executes these bounded, multi-step units under an **autonomy envelope** — maximum tier, permitted tools, spend caps — with every step recorded. *Derived from:* "governed" (the envelope is enforcement, not convention) and "one governance for both solution kinds" (Way 1 and Way 2 skills execute on the same runtime; an embedded component cannot have ungoverned internals). The execution ladder above it: the Model Access makes one call; the Skill Runtime runs one skill; the Agent Case Framework (Layer 2) orchestrates one case across humans and time. Way 2 stops at the second rung.

**AI Memory** — *what the AI draws on.* Three governed holdings: the **knowledge base** (versioned, curated content that shapes AI behavior — procedures, regulatory texts, billing rules — activated only through evaluation gates, so the AI never quietly works from unapproved sources); **context assembly** (the service that composes what a model sees for a task — case data, evidence, reference data, knowledge — under the caller's entitlements, with the composition recorded); and the **evidence store** (documents and artifacts, content-addressed, immutable, retention-policied, with field-level provenance from extraction). *Derived from:* "attestable" — the question every supervisor will eventually ask, *what did the AI know when it decided?*, has an exact, recorded answer for every single decision.

**AI Auditor** — *what happened, provably.* The append-only, hash-chained ledger of every meaningful action — by model, system, or human — plus the human-readable journey per case and packaged audit exports (timeline, approvals, evidence bundle, change log). The platform's single most important invariant lives here: **nothing changes state or causes a side effect except through a recorded execution.** *Derived from:* "attestable" and "accountability made carryable" — the customer answers the auditor from primary evidence the platform produced as a by-product of doing the work. Named an auditor, not a log, deliberately: it reconstructs, verifies its own chain, and testifies.

**Evaluation Engine** — *how good it is, on evidence.* Golden datasets (curated, versioned reference cases with known-correct outcomes), backtests, regression gates on every change, drift detection on live traffic. *Derived from:* "earned autonomy" (the evidence that "earned" refers to is produced here) and "no silent changes" (no prompt, policy, knowledge version, or playbook ships without passing its declared thresholds — which travel inside the playbook artifact itself). It operated in two layers evaluations on reconfiguration (e.g. new model upgrade) and evaluation during runtime (live evaluations, monthly audits and quarterly reviews).

**Learning Engine** — *how it gets better, governedly.* Captures corrections, overrides, and outcomes as structured signals; curates them into datasets and golden-set candidates; proposes changes to prompts, policies, and playbooks; releases exclusively through the Evaluation Engine's gates. *Derived from:* "getting better every cycle" — and from the operating model: this engine is what NXT operates as a service across all deployments; it is the run in design-build-run.

**Policy Gate** — *may this happen, now.* The customer's approval matrices, thresholds, escalation routes, and segregation-of-duties rules as versioned, executable rule sets evaluated at defined checkpoints — before any side effect. Every evaluation is itself recorded. *Derived from:* "governed" — policies are gates the platform enforces, not documents the organization hopes are followed.

**Autonomy Controller** — *what this capability may do at all, and what it has earned.* Per capability, per tenant: the autonomy tier (draft → review → sample → auto), bound to evaluation evidence, raised only through approved, recorded promotion — and lowered automatically the moment quality slips. Computes the envelopes the Skill Runtime enforces. *Derived from:* the flagship claim — autonomy is a status earned on evidence, not a setting. The Policy Gate answers the situational question; the Autonomy Controller answers the standing one. Both must hold.

**Integration Fabric** — *the customer's systems, touched correctly.* Three connector tiers: ingestion (mail, upload, API), reference reads (typed, with mandatory freshness contracts — stale data raises an exception, never a silent risk, and the freshness of data used in any decision is recorded in that decision's context), and handoffs (write-backs — idempotency-keyed, policy-gated, recorded). Failures degrade safely into visible exceptions; work continues under human review. *Derived from:* "on top of your systems" — the customer's systems remain the systems of record; the platform never builds shadow masters.

**Platform Core** — *the unglamorous preconditions.* Identity (federation to the customer's IdP — Entra ID first; machine credentials per service and partner), hard tenant isolation by workspace, configuration management, and technical observability (throughput, health, alerting — the plumbing). *Derived from:* "your environment" and enterprise IT reality. Deliberately the last element: AI observability is not a box here — it is the kernel itself; the Auditor, the Evaluation Engine, and AI Memory together are what "it sees and remembers" means. Platform Core only watches the machines.

## 4. Layer 2 — the AI Suites, element by element

**Platform API** — the single external surface: open, versioned, documented. Everything the suites can do is reachable through it, and NXT's own surfaces consume it without private backdoors — if our Workspace can live with the public API, a partner can. Layers 3 and 4 expose at most named service endpoints as thin façades over it. *Derived from:* "two kinds of solutions" (embedding requires a real API product) and honesty (one surface, one contract, one audit of access).

**The three human surfaces.** **NXT Workspace** — humans *in* the loop: queues, evidence-first review (the AI's proposal with its reasoning and documents beside it), one-click approve, reject, correct, override — every action recorded, every correction a learning signal. **NXT Cockpit** — humans *on* the loop: per-capability quality scorecards, sampling of completed work, drift review, and the place where autonomy promotions are decided on evidence. Oversight as a workflow, not a quarterly meeting. **NXT Admin** — customer administrators: users and roles, policy values, autonomy tiers, connector credentials, branding — the user interface onto Layer 4 configuration, with every change recorded like any other action. *Derived from:* "your team in command" — in the loop and on the loop are different jobs, different screens, both first-class. (A fourth surface, the Platform Console, is NXT-internal and never customer-visible: fleet quality across environments, release promotion, model routing — the tooling with which NXT operates the AI.)

**Way 1 — Case Operations.** For regulated case operations end to end. **Agent Case Framework:** the orchestration of one case across state, humans, and time — typed cases with lifecycles, SLAs and ownership; work items; exception management with resolution flows; human checkpoints; the case-level agents that decide next steps, orchestrating through the framework while executing every skill on the kernel's runtime, so nothing agentic bypasses recording. **Case Domain Memory:** the operational records — cases, work items, exceptions, approvals — the data with which an operation answers "where do we stand?" at any moment. **Case Execution Library:** the proven operational building blocks — the Inbox (multi-channel intake, deduplication, deterministic routing plus confidence-thresholded classification, identity resolution), Routing and assignment, the Communication engine (draft → polish → send & track, replies threading back), and Case Analytics (volumes, cycle times, SLA, automation share — from the same records the Auditor trusts, so the management view and the audit view never disagree).

**Way 2 — Embedded AI Components.** For governed AI inside the customer's software and workflows. **Component Registry:** the catalogue of deployable components with their contracts, versions, and entitlements. **Component Memory:** each component's own governed state and history — what it has processed, learned signals, its quality series. **Service Contract & SDKs:** the standard call shape — submit → assess → result with explanation, confidence, and evidence link — idempotent, asynchronous-first, with TypeScript and Python SDKs generated from the API; every call creates a case, produces executions, writes the same ledger. **Metering:** billing-grade usage counting per tenant and component — the commercial meter for embedded business. *Derived from:* "an embedded call is governed exactly like a case on a screen" — Way 2 carries no case orchestration (the execution ladder ends at the Skill Runtime), but it carries the full governance.

## 5. Layer 3 — AI-native Playbooks, anatomy

A playbook is the versioned, executable description of how a class of work gets done. Inside the artifact: the **process design** (the AI-native flow — which steps, which skills, where humans decide, drawn from a small deterministic vocabulary so a process owner and an auditor can read it); the **skills** with their prompts; the **golden datasets** owned by this playbook; the **policy templates** with typed parameters (values bind at Layer 4 — a deployment may bind parameters, never patch the playbook); the **exception taxonomy** with resolution flows; the **Workspace checkpoints** (the views and review steps of its human side); and the **release gate** — the evaluation suites and thresholds a version must pass before any environment may run it. Publishing is therefore a quality event; activation per environment is an explicit, recorded promotion.

Playbooks package into **modules** — playbooks plus connector mappings, audit export templates, documentation, rollout playbook, and a pricing unit. Current portfolio: Complaint Management / Bestandsautomatisierung (Banking) in build with the first customer, productized from day one for the second; Claims Management (Insurance) as a candidate; Founders Finance as the internal vertical and validation bed (payment execution quarantined there: hard-flagged, internal-only, absent from every customer surface).

## 6. Layer 4 — Deployment, anatomy

**Configuration:** policy values, autonomy tier states, mappings, branding, connector credentials, playbook parameter bindings — managed in NXT Admin, exportable as one reviewable bundle (the basis of instance reproducibility and safe change). **Customer components:** solutions built for one customer on published Layer 1/2 contracts — owned by or exclusive to the customer, composed of skills, fully governed by the kernel: audited, evaluation-gated, learning like everything else. The defining case: a billing-completeness component built for a software vendor and sold inside their product — never an NXT product, while the platform beneath remains NXT's, licensed, including in resale.

The **IP axis** is read directly off the layers: Platform (NXT, always — L1/L2), Product modules and playbooks (NXT, multi-customer — L3), Customer components and configuration (customer-owned or exclusive — L4). Promotion from L4 to L3 is governed: tenant-parameterization, an own golden set with evaluation baseline, published contracts and documentation, and a cleared IP and commercial check. Generic learnings flow down freely; domain assets move only through this gate.

## 7. How it deploys and operates

**In the customer's environment — bring your own compute.** One self-contained, Kubernetes-based instance per customer, on their infrastructure, inside their perimeter, federated to their identity, storing to their object store, forwarding metrics into their monitoring, riding their change process — with NXT's evaluation evidence attached to every release as input to their change advisory board. Instances are reproducible from configuration: one platform, many instances, never forks.

**The operating split: the customer operates the system; NXT operates the AI.** The customer runs infrastructure and first-level operations. NXT provides second-level platform support and operates the quality cycle — evaluation, drift response, golden-set curation, prompt and playbook evolution, autonomy management — through the Learning and Evaluation Engines, from outside the perimeter, on the **telemetry export**: a schema-versioned, customer-inspectable allow-list of quality signals (scores, metrics, error signatures — never business content). The export pipeline is the operating model's lifeline and is therefore a named architectural element, contractually mirrored in a standard operating annex.

**Three integration modes** govern coexistence with what the customer already runs — flexible about where flows run, non-negotiable about how AI decisions are governed: **Mode A**, NXT runs the process end to end (Way 1); **Mode B**, the customer's existing orchestration or product calls NXT components as governed steps (Way 2 — technically a thin worker speaking the service contract; days of integration); **Mode C**, split — NXT runs the AI-native segment, deterministic tails hand off through the Integration Fabric.

**SaaS is deliberately not built.** The door is held open by three decisions already present — workspace-scoped everything, instance-from-configuration, billing-grade metering — and nothing more is built behind it until a product decision exists.

## 8. Build vs. borrow

NXT owns everything that carries the guarantees and the playbook IP; everything beneath is proven open-source infrastructure, self-hosted in the deployment's perimeter. Proprietary, never substituted: the ten kernel elements' governance semantics, the playbook format, the service and connector contracts. Adopted deliberately: PostgreSQL, Redis, Kubernetes-stack operations, Keycloak-class identity federation, an OSS LLM proxy beneath the Model Access language backend, an OSS model-serving runtime (BentoML/KServe-class) beneath its ML backend, a Langfuse-class substrate beneath the Evaluation Engine (the governance on top stays ours), pgvector beneath AI Memory's retrieval, OSS parsers beneath the provenance layer, the customer's own S3-compatible store. Deliberately not adopted: external workflow engines (case orchestration is too close to the product's core; coexistence is solved by Mode B, not by internal adoption), standalone policy engines (policy evaluation stays fused with audit and autonomy), heavyweight ELT frameworks (wrong abstraction for freshness-contracted mirrors). Every adoption passes a license gate at adoption time — instances ship into customer perimeters.

## 9. Vision

NXT Core is the AI Operations Platform: the governed execution layer on which AI works in regulated industries — running proven playbooks on top of existing systems, in the customer's own environment, never replacing what already works. Every decision is recorded with what the AI saw. Every change is validated before release. Every autonomy level is earned on evidence. The customer's team stays in command; the customer's accountability is answered from evidence; and every deployment makes the next one faster.
